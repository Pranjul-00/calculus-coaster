Track Design: Piecewise Functions & C¬π Continuity

How we mathematically designed a smooth, thrilling roller coaster track using advanced calculus concepts and continuity analysis.

Why Piecewise Functions?

A realistic track cannot be modeled by a single simple function. Our track is designed by connecting four separate mathematical functions (a "piecewise" function).

Characteristics: Gently rises from the valley exit and controls landing height and speed before launch into projectile motion.

üîó C¬π Continuity: The Key to Smooth Rides

The most critical part of the design is making the track smooth. If the pieces just "meet," they would create a sharp "kink."

The Problem:

Simply connecting four functions at their endpoints would create dangerous sharp corners that could:

  • ‚ùå Cause uncomfortable jolts for riders
  • ‚ùå Create infinite G-forces at connection points
  • ‚ùå Make the track physically impossible to build

The Solution: C¬π Continuity

We enforced C¬π Continuity, a mathematical condition requiring two things at every connection point:

1. Height Continuity

$$f_1(x) = f_2(x) \text{ at connection points}$$

The heights are equal - no sudden jumps up or down

2. Slope Continuity

$$f'_1(x) = f'_2(x) \text{ at connection points}$$

The slopes are equal - no sharp corners or kinks

‚úÖ The Result:

This ensures a seamless transition with no sharp corners that could be dangerous or uncomfortable. The track flows smoothly from piece to piece, just like a real roller coaster.

üéØ Launch Geometry: Handing Off to Projectile Motion

The final part of the track is designed so the cart can safely "take off" into the air and follow the projectile equations described on the Theory and Physics pages.

Exit Height and Slope:

At the end of the track, two design choices matter most:

  • Height at exit: The value of $f(x)$ at the last point becomes the launch height $y_0$.
  • Slope at exit: The derivative $f'(x)$ at that point sets the launch angle, which splits the speed into horizontal $v_{x0}$ and vertical $v_{y0}$ components.

Together with the speed computed from energy conservation, these values form the initial conditions $(x_0, y_0, v_{x0}, v_{y0})$ for the projectile motion.

Design Goals for the Launch:

  • Keep the exit ramp smooth and continuous with the rest of the track (no sudden kinks).
  • Avoid a perfectly vertical slope so that $v_{x0}$ is not zero and the flight path is a clear, long arc.
  • Choose a height and angle that produce a visually satisfying flight before the cart lands.

Connection to the Simulation:

The p5.js code reads the final track point and its slope to compute $x_0, y_0, v_{x0}, v_{y0}$. These feed directly into the projectile equations $x(t)$, $y(t)$ and $y(x)$ shown beneath the simulation canvas.
