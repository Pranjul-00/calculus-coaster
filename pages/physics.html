Physics Model: Realistic Motion

How conservation of energy creates authentic roller coaster physics through mathematical modeling and real-time calculations.

Conservation of Energy: The Foundation

The animation is not pre-set. It's a live physics simulation governed by the Conservation of Energy.

The Principle:

We assume the cart starts at height hstart (with a tiny "push") and trades its Potential Energy (PE) for Kinetic Energy (KE).

$$PE_{initial} = PE_{current} + KE_{current}$$
$$mgh_{start} = mgh + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Maximum potential energy, minimum kinetic energy (slow speed)

üèéÔ∏è In the Valley

Minimum potential energy, maximum kinetic energy (high speed)

üöÄ Velocity (Speed): v(x)

By solving the energy equation for v (velocity), we get the master formula for the cart's speed at any point.

Master Equation:

$$v(x) = \sqrt{2g(h_{start} - f(x))}$$

The "Four Equations":

Because f(x) has four different formulas, this master equation results in four different, specific velocity equations:

$$v_1(x) = \sqrt{2g(h_{start} - f_1(x))}$$
$$v_2(x) = \sqrt{2g(h_{start} - f_2(x))}$$
$$v_3(x) = \sqrt{2g(h_{start} - f_3(x))}$$
$$v_4(x) = \sqrt{2g(h_{start} - f_4(x))}$$

üéÆ In the Simulation:

Our code calculates this value 60 times per second to find the cart's true speed. This is why it speeds up in the valleys and slows down on the hills - pure physics!

‚ö° G-Forces: Gs(x)

The "apparent weight" a rider feels. It's a combination of Earth's gravity (1 G) and the centripetal acceleration from the curve.

Master Equation:

$$Gs(x) = \frac{1 + [f'(x)]^2 + 2(h_{start} - f(x)) \cdot f''(x)}{(1 + [f'(x)]^2)^{3/2}}$$

What Riders Feel:

üèîÔ∏è Valley Bottom (x=10)

f''(x) is positive ‚Üí Gs > 1

"Pressed into your seat" feeling

üéà Hill Top (x=20)

f''(x) is negative ‚Üí Gs < 1

"Airtime" or weightless feeling

G-Force Scale:

  • 1 G: Normal Earth gravity
  • 2-3 G: Intense but safe (pressed into seat)
  • 0.5 G: Light, floating sensation
  • 0 G: Complete weightlessness (airtime)

üéØ Projectile Flight After the Track

After the cart leaves the final hill, it is no longer constrained by the track and follows pure projectile motion under gravity.

Launch Conditions:

At the last point on the track, we record the cart's position and velocity components:

$$ x(0) = x_0, \quad y(0) = y_0, \quad v_x(0) = v_{x0}, \quad v_y(0) = v_{y0} $$

The values $x_0$, $y_0$, $v_{x0}$, and $v_{y0}$ come from the energy-based speed and the track slope at the launch point.

Time-Parametric Equations x(t) and y(t):

Once in the air, horizontal acceleration is zero and vertical acceleration is $-g$, so the motion is governed by:

$$ x(t) = x_0 + v_{x0} t, \quad y(t) = y_0 + v_{y0} t - \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2 $$

The simulation updates these equations frame by frame and uses them to draw the dotted flight path.

Cartesian Flight Path y(x):

When the horizontal velocity component is non-zero ($v_{x0} \neq 0$), we can rewrite the motion as a single function of $x$:

$$ y(x) = y_0 + \frac{v_{y0}}{v_{x0}} (x - x_0) - \frac{g}{2 v_{x0}^2} (x - x_0)^2 $$

This downward-opening parabola is the equation shown under the simulation canvas during flight.

If $v_{x0} \approx 0$ (a nearly vertical launch), $x(t) \approx x_0$ and it is not meaningful to write $y$ as a single-valued function of $x$, so the simulation only reports the parametric form.

üéÆ In the Simulation:

As soon as the cart leaves the track, the track-based G-force and $v(x)$ formulas stop applying. The motion is now governed entirely by gravity and the launch velocity components, exactly matching the projectile equations described on the Theory page.

üîÑ Real-time Physics Simulation

Every frame of our simulation involves complex calculations happening 60 times per second.

Step 1: Position

Calculate current height using f(x)

Step 2: Derivatives

Find slope f'(x) and concavity f''(x)

Step 3: Velocity

Apply energy conservation formula

Step 4: G-Forces

Calculate apparent weight from curvature

Step 5: Motion

Update cart position for next frame

Why This Matters:

This isn't just animation - it's a genuine physics simulation. The cart behaves exactly as it would in real life, following the same laws of physics that govern actual roller coasters.
