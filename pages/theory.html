Theoretical Framework

A rigorous examination of the differential and integral calculus governing particle motion along a constrained piecewise path.

1. The Geometry of the Path

The roller coaster track is modeled as a continuous piecewise function $f(x)$ defined on the domain $x \in [0, 35]$. The function relies on polynomial interpolation to achieve specific geometric constraints.

The Piecewise Definition

$$ f(x) = \begin{cases} 20 - 0.32 x^2 & \text{if } 0 \le x < 5 \\ 0.0064 (x-10)^4 + 8.0 & \text{if } 5 \le x < 15 \\ 20.0 - 0.32 (x-20)^2 & \text{if } 15 \le x < 25 \\ 0.32 (x-30)^2 + 4.0 & \text{if } 25 \le x \le 35 \end{cases} $$

Derivatives & Gradients

The first derivative $f'(x)$ provides the instantaneous slope, which is crucial for determining the tangent vector $\vec{T}$.

$$ f'(x) = \begin{cases} -0.64 x & 0 \le x < 5 \\ 0.0256 (x-10)^3 & 5 \le x < 15 \\ -0.64 (x-20) & 15 \le x < 25 \\ 0.64 (x-30) & 25 \le x \le 35 \end{cases} $$

2. Continuity Analysis ($C^0$ and $C^1$)

For the ride to be safe, the track must not have gaps ($C^0$ continuous) or sharp corners ($C^1$ continuous). We prove this using limits at the critical junction $x=5$.

Proof of $C^0$ Continuity (Position) at $x=5$

We evaluate the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches 5 from the left and right.

$$ \lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) = 20 - 0.32(5)^2 = 20 - 8 = 12 $$
$$ \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) = 0.0064(5-10)^4 + 8 = 0.0064(-5)^4 + 8 = 12 $$

Since $\lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) = 12$, the track is connected.

Proof of $C^1$ Continuity (Differentiability) at $x=5$

We evaluate the limit of the derivative $f'(x)$ to ensure the slope is smooth.

$$ \lim_{x \to 5^-} f'(x) = -0.64(5) = -3.2 $$
$$ \lim_{x \to 5^+} f'(x) = 0.0256(5-10)^3 = 0.0256(-125) = -3.2 $$

Since the left and right derivatives match, the tangent line is unique, and the ride is smooth.

3. Integral Calculus: Arc Length & Time

We utilize Definite Integrals to calculate physical properties of the path that cannot be found via simple algebra.

The Arc Length Integral ($s$)

To find the total physical length of the track, we treat the curve as an infinite sum of hypotenuses $ds$ formed by $dx$ and $dy$.

$$ ds = \sqrt{dx^2 + dy^2} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2} \, dx $$

Integrating over the domain $x \in [a, b]$:

$$ S = \int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} \, dx $$

The Time Integral ($T$)

Time is distance divided by speed. Since speed $v(x)$ varies continuously with height, we must integrate the infinitesimal time element $dt$.

$$ v = \frac{ds}{dt} \implies dt = \frac{ds}{v(x)} $$

Substituting $ds$ and the velocity equation derived from energy conservation ($v(x) = \sqrt{2g(H-y)}$):

$$ T_{total} = \int_{0}^{35} \frac{\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}}{\sqrt{2g(H_{start} - f(x))}} \, dx $$

This integral typically has no closed-form solution for 4th-order polynomials and is solved numerically in the simulation using Riemann Sums.

4. Vector Calculus & Curvature

To determine the G-forces, we must analyze the change in the direction of the velocity vector. This requires the Radius of Curvature $\rho$.

Deriving Curvature ($\kappa$)

Curvature is the rate of change of the tangent vector angle $\phi$ with respect to arc length $s$.

$$ \kappa = \frac{d\phi}{ds} = \frac{d\phi/dx}{ds/dx} $$

Using $\tan \phi = f'(x)$, we derive:

$$ \kappa(x) = \frac{f''(x)}{(1 + [f'(x)]^2)^{3/2}} $$

Normal Force (G-Force) Vector

The force exerted by the track on the cart ($N$) is the sum of the gravitational component perpendicular to the track and the centripetal force.

$$ \vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a} $$
$$ N - mg \cos \theta = \frac{mv^2}{\rho} $$

Solving for G-Force ($N/mg$):

$$ Gs = \cos \theta + \frac{v^2}{g \rho} $$

5. Projectile Motion (Differential Equations)

Once the cart leaves the track ($x > 35$), the constraints are removed. The motion is defined by a system of second-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs).

System of ODEs

$$ m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = 0 $$
$$ m \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -mg $$

Solution (Parametric Equations)

Integrating twice with respect to time $t$ yields the equations of motion used in the simulation:

$$ x(t) = v_{0x}t + x_0 $$
$$ y(t) = -\frac{1}{2}gt^2 + v_{0y}t + y_0 $$

Cartesian Trajectory Equation

Eliminating $t$ yields the parabolic path equation in terms of $x$:

$$ y(x) = y_0 + \tan(\theta_0)(x - x_0) - \frac{g(x - x_0)^2}{2v_0^2 \cos^2(\theta_0)} $$
