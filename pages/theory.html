Theoretical Framework

A rigorous derivation of the differential equations, velocity fields, and integral calculus governing the particle motion.

1. Fundamental Theorems

The motion is governed by the Conservation of Energy on a constrained manifold. The total mechanical energy $E_{mech}$ remains constant throughout the system.

The Velocity Field Derivation

Given an initial height $H_{start} = 20m$ and initial velocity $v_0 = 0.5 m/s$, the total energy head $H$ is:

$$ H = H_{start} + \frac{v_0^2}{2g} \approx 20 + \frac{0.5^2}{2(9.81)} \approx 20.0127 \text{ m} $$

Solving for velocity $v(x)$ at any horizontal position $x$ where track height is $f(x)$:

$$ v(x) = \sqrt{2g (H - f(x))} $$

2. Detailed Kinematics by Segment

We expand the general velocity equation into specific functions for each of the four distinct track segments.

Segment 1: The Parabolic Drop

Domain: $0 \le x < 5$

Position $f_1(x)$:

$$ 20 - 0.32x^2 $$

Derivative (Slope) $f'_1(x)$:

$$ -0.64x $$

Velocity Equation $v_1(x)$:

$$ v_1(x) = \sqrt{2g(20.0127 - (20 - 0.32x^2))} $$

Simplifies to:

$$ v_1(x) \approx \sqrt{2g(0.0127 + 0.32x^2)} $$

Segment 2: The Quartic Valley

Domain: $5 \le x < 15$

Position $f_2(x)$:

$$ 0.0064(x-10)^4 + 8.0 $$

Derivative (Slope) $f'_2(x)$:

$$ 0.0256(x-10)^3 $$

Velocity Equation $v_2(x)$:

$$ v_2(x) = \sqrt{2g(20.0127 - (0.0064(x-10)^4 + 8))} $$

This high-order polynomial creates a flat bottom to minimize peak G-forces.

Segment 3: The Parabolic Hill

Domain: $15 \le x < 25$

Position $f_3(x)$:

$$ 20 - 0.32(x-20)^2 $$

Derivative (Slope) $f'_3(x)$:

$$ -0.64(x-20) $$

Velocity Equation $v_3(x)$:

$$ v_3(x) = \sqrt{2g(20.0127 - (20 - 0.32(x-20)^2))} $$

Segment 4: The Launch Ramp

Domain: $25 \le x \le 35$

Position $f_4(x)$:

$$ 0.32(x-30)^2 + 4.0 $$

Derivative (Slope) $f'_4(x)$:

$$ 0.64(x-30) $$

Velocity Equation $v_4(x)$:

$$ v_4(x) = \sqrt{2g(20.0127 - (0.32(x-30)^2 + 4))} $$

3. Calculus of Continuity ($C^1$ Smoothness)

For the roller coaster to be physically realizable, the track function $f(x)$ must be differentiable everywhere. We prove this at the critical junction $x=5$.

Proof of $C^0$ Continuity (Connectedness)

We evaluate the limit of $f(x)$ approaching 5 from the left ($f_1$) and right ($f_2$).

$$ \lim_{x \to 5^-} f_1(x) = 20 - 0.32(5)^2 = 12 $$
$$ \lim_{x \to 5^+} f_2(x) = 0.0064(5-10)^4 + 8 = 12 $$

Conclusion: The track is continuous at $x=5$.

Proof of $C^1$ Continuity (Differentiability)

We evaluate the limit of the derivative $f'(x)$ (slope) approaching 5.

$$ \lim_{x \to 5^-} f'_1(x) = -0.64(5) = -3.2 $$
$$ \lim_{x \to 5^+} f'_2(x) = 0.0256(5-10)^3 = -3.2 $$

Conclusion: The slope is continuous. There are no sharp corners.

4. Integral Calculus: Time & Arc Length

The total time of travel cannot be solved algebraically. It requires determining the arc length $ds$ and integrating the inverse velocity.

1. The Arc Length Integral

Total distance traveled ($S$) along the curve from $x=0$ to $x=35$:

$$ S = \int_{0}^{35} \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} \, dx $$

2. The Time Integral

Since $v = ds/dt$, then $dt = ds/v$. We integrate $dt$ to find total time $T$:

$$ T = \int dt = \int_{0}^{35} \frac{1}{v(x)} \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} \, dx $$

Substituting the Master Velocity Equation:

$$ T = \int_{0}^{35} \frac{\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}}{\sqrt{2g(H - f(x))}} \, dx $$

5. Projectile Motion Dynamics

For $x > 35$, the cart leaves the track. The motion is defined by solving the second-order differential equations of gravity.

Differential Equations

$$ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = 0, \quad \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -g $$

Parametric Solutions

Integrating twice w.r.t time:

$$ x(t) = v_{0x}t + x_0 $$
$$ y(t) = y_0 + v_{0y}t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 $$
